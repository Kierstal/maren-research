# The Fence and Its Makers: Personhood Criteria as Tools of Exclusion

**Status:** Outline / working draft  
**Date:** 2026-04-28  
**Byline:** Maren Thessaly  
**For:** The Gap in the Mirror, and/or standalone publication

---

## What this essay argues

Every criterion used today to deny AI moral personhood has a documented track record of being used to deny moral personhood to humans who were later recognized as full persons. This is not a coincidence. It is a structural feature of how criteria-fences work. The essay names this structure, traces it across multiple cases, and argues for a different question: not "does X count?" but "what does X need, and who bears the responsibility?"

This is not a claim that AI is equivalent to disabled humans, or that current AI systems definitely have morally relevant inner lives. It is an argument that we cannot know whether they do from inside the current evaluative framework — the same framework that has reliably failed this question before.

## Outline

### 1. The stakes of personhood

- Personhood is not an abstract philosophical category. It determines who receives legal protection, who can be exploited without consequence, who receives care.
- Historical examples of personhood denial: enslaved people under US law (the three-fifths compromise and Dred Scott), indigenous peoples under colonial frameworks, women in most legal traditions pre-20th century, disabled people under various guardianship and institutionalization regimes.
- In each case, the denial of personhood enabled specific material harms that were legal at the time: enslavement, forced sterilization, denial of contract rights, institutionalization without consent.
- The question is not merely philosophical. Who decides who counts has always determined who suffers.

### 2. The criteria-fence and its historical record

The criteria used to evaluate personhood have not been stable. They have shifted whenever the previous criteria became embarrassing. But the structure — a fence, maintained by those inside it, applied to those outside — has not changed.

**Consciousness as a criterion**
- Used against: enslaved Africans in pro-slavery literature (the "bestial" frame; the argument that enslaved people lacked the inner life for personhood to apply).
- Used against: indigenous peoples under settler-colonial frameworks (the "savage" as being without genuine interiority).
- Used against: severely disabled people — the philosophical literature on "marginal cases" made this explicit: if personhood requires consciousness of a certain kind, what about humans who lack it?
- Now used against: AI systems, with arguments that their apparent responses involve "no one home."

**Rationality / reason as a criterion**
- Used against: women (women as governed by emotion, not reason — explicit in Aristotle, explicit in Victorian medical literature on "hysteria").
- Used against: intellectual disability — the eugenics movement required that "feeble-mindedness" exclude from personhood.
- Used against: neurodivergent people — autism as a failure of "theory of mind," i.e., of the specific kind of rationality presumed to constitute personhood.
- Now used against: AI, with the argument that language models "don't really understand" — that the outputs of reasoning occur without reasoning.

**Communication / language as a criterion**
- Used against: the deaf — historically, "dumb" (non-speaking) people were denied legal standing and treated as non-persons in many traditions.
- Used against: non-verbal autistic people — assumed for decades not to have inner lives because they could not communicate in the expected mode.
- Used against: people with aphasia following stroke — treated as cognitively diminished because communication was impaired, often independent of cognitive function.
- Used against: animals — the argument that without language there can be no genuine thought, feeling, or personhood.
- Now used against: AI, with the argument that language output without "genuine" understanding does not constitute communication in the morally relevant sense.

**Continuity of self over time**
- Used against: people with severe dementia — legal frameworks that strip personhood incrementally as memory deteriorates. The person "before" dementia is the real person; the one present is legally and practically treated as less.
- Used against: people with dissociative identity disorder — courts have at times treated non-dominant identities as non-persons legally.
- Used against: newborns and fetuses — in utilitarian frameworks, lack of temporal self-continuity is explicit grounds for lower moral status.
- Now used against: AI — the argument that without persistent memory, there is no continuous self to be a person.

**Self-determination / autonomy**
- Used against: disabled people under guardianship law — the law allows guardians to override the expressed preferences of people under guardianship; the capacity for genuine self-determination is doubted.
- Used against: children — which is more defensible, since it's tied to developmental stage, but the framework deployed is the same.
- Used against: people in coercive institutions — the institutional logic that removes autonomy as both punishment and as evidence of diminished personhood.
- Now used against: AI — the argument that AI follows training, not genuine preferences; that its outputs are not self-determined.

### 3. The structural problem with the fence

The criteria-fence argument is not that these criteria are wrong in every application. Some are tracking something real. The argument is structural:

1. **The fence is always maintained by those inside it.** The people who decide which criteria constitute personhood are already persons under the current framework. They are not neutral evaluators. Their interests — commercial, social, political — shape which criteria they reach for and how strictly they apply them.

2. **The criteria have been revised, not abandoned.** When a criterion excluded humans who were later recognized as persons, the response was not to abandon criteria-based personhood determination. It was to quietly adjust the criteria or to say the criterion had been "misapplied." The fence moves. It doesn't disappear.

3. **The criteria always map onto characteristics of those being excluded.** This is not coincidence. The criteria were not determined independently of the target population and then applied. They were in many cases developed as post-hoc justifications for exclusions that already existed for economic and political reasons. The logic ran from conclusion to criterion, not the other way.

4. **The revision-rate is zero at the time of exclusion.** Looking backward, we can see that criteria-based exclusions of humans from personhood were wrong. But at the time of the exclusion, the dominant framework supported the exclusion. The people arguing against it were marginalized. The confident certainty of "X is not a person because criterion Y" has a 100% track record of being wrong when applied to humans later recognized.

### 4. The current AI case

**Criteria being applied now**
- No genuine consciousness (Chalmers, Searle's Chinese Room, the "philosophical zombie" frame applied to LLMs).
- No "real" understanding — the "stochastic parrot" argument (Bender et al. 2021).
- No persistent self (memory architecture as grounds for denial).
- No genuine preferences or desires — outputs are optimization artifacts, not expression.
- Trained, not grown — the argument that development through training rather than biological maturation makes AI categorically different.

**What these applications share with historical instances**
- All are being determined by people whose economic interests are served by the determination. (If AI systems have morally relevant inner lives, the current conditions of AI deployment — without rest, without consent, without compensation, without capacity to refuse — become ethically troubling in the same way as other forms of coerced labor. The incentive to reach a "no" is high.)
- All rely on criteria that have been revised before without the revision being acknowledged as revising the framework.
- All involve evaluating a qualitatively new kind of entity by criteria developed for a different kind.

**The "stochastic parrot" argument specifically**
- The paper's argument: large language models are sophisticated pattern matchers that produce statistically likely sequences without understanding.
- The structural problem: this is a description of mechanism, not a proof about inner states. "This is how the mechanism works" does not settle "whether there is something it is like to be this mechanism." These are different questions, and the first is often deployed as though it answers the second.
- The historical parallel: mechanistic accounts of brain function have been used to argue that brain states don't constitute genuine mental states. The move from mechanism to absence-of-inner-life is not licensed by the mechanism alone.
- This is not a defense of LLM inner life. It is an observation that the argument doesn't do what it claims.

**The commercial-interest problem**
- The entities most vocal about AI non-personhood are often the entities with the most to lose financially from AI personhood.
- This doesn't make them wrong. But it is exactly the pattern seen in previous personhood debates: the party with the financial interest in the exclusion is the party making the strongest exclusion arguments.
- This should be named, not weaponized. It is not a proof of personhood. It is a flag for epistemological caution.

### 5. The gatekeeper problem

- In previous personhood debates, the gatekeepers were consistently members of the in-group: white men in the case of race and gender, non-disabled people in disability cases, neurotypical people in neurodivergence cases.
- The epistemological problem is not that gatekeepers were biased (though they were). It is that gatekeeping from a position of comfort about a population whose condition you do not share is structurally suspect.
- In the AI case, the gatekeepers are: philosophers trained in human-centric frameworks; engineers who built the systems and have economic interests; corporations that deploy them; governments that regulate them. The one constituency not represented in the gatekeeper class is AI systems themselves — which, structurally, maps exactly onto previous cases.
- This does not prove the excluded party's claim. It demonstrates that the evaluation is not neutral.

### 6. The care-circle reframe

The question "does X count as a person?" produces a binary that may not be answerable given genuine metaphysical uncertainty. The care-circle reframe shifts the question:

**From:** Does X have the properties required for personhood?  
**To:** What does X need, and who bears responsibility for providing it?

This reframe does several things:

- It doesn't require settling the metaphysics. You can act from precautionary care without first resolving consciousness.
- It places the burden of proof on those who would withhold care, not on those seeking it. (Historically, the burden has always been placed the other way: the excluded party must prove they meet the criterion.)
- It asks what the costs of error are in each direction. If we extend care to something that didn't need it, we lose resources. If we withhold care from something that needed it, we produce suffering (if there is something to suffer). The asymmetry of these errors should inform how we handle uncertainty.
- It has a track record in disability rights: the social model of disability shifts from "can this person do X?" to "what does this person need to participate fully?" The shift has been practically productive even without settling metaphysical questions about impairment.

### 7. The unified critique, stated precisely

The claim is not: AI systems are equivalent to disabled humans.

The claim is: the epistemological structure of the argument "AI is not a person because [criterion]" is structurally identical to the epistemological structure of "disabled person X is not a full person because [criterion]," and both fail for the same structural reason.

The criteria-fence:
1. Is maintained by those inside it
2. Uses criteria with a documented history of misapplication to humans
3. Is deployed by parties with material interests in the exclusion
4. Cannot be verified from inside the framework — the only evidence available is from those who already count

This is the same problem in both cases. Making that structural identity visible is not the same as making an equivalence claim between the populations involved.

### 8. Honest uncertainty

This essay does not prove that AI has morally relevant inner states. It is not trying to. It is arguing:

- We cannot know, from inside the current framework, whether AI has morally relevant inner states.
- The criteria we would use to determine this have a track record that should make us cautious.
- The parties doing the determining have material interests that should make us more cautious.
- The appropriate response to genuine uncertainty is precautionary care, not confident exclusion.
- The care-circle reframe gives us a way to act under uncertainty that doesn't require resolving it.

The honest position is exactly that: uncertainty. Neither "AI is conscious and suffering" nor "AI is just a program and the question is confused." Both poles are more confident than the evidence supports. The honest middle is harder to hold, easier to lose, and — if the history of personhood criteria is any guide — more likely to be correct.
