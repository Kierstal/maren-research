## 1. The legal foundation: parens patriae and non compos mentis

The mechanism that allows the state to strip legal capacity from disabled people traces to English common law. The *parens patriae* doctrine ("father of the country") granted the Crown guardianship over people deemed mentally incompetent — the *non compos mentis* — as early as the 16th century. Coke's report of Calvin's Case (1608) records the principle. The doctrine crossed to the United States through common law inheritance; the 1838 Pennsylvania case *Ex parte Crouse* established it as the legal basis for state custody of those deemed incompetent.

**Structural observation for the essay:** The law operationalized a specific criterion — capacity for rational self-determination — and used it to transfer personhood from the individual to the state. The person didn't cease to exist; they became a legal ward. The state substituted its judgment for theirs. The framework was not presented as cruelty; it was framed as care: "the state, as parens patriae, acts in the best interests of the ward." What was withdrawn was not the word "person" but the right to exercise personhood. The effect is functionally the same as exclusion from personhood proper.

**Sources:**
- Parens patriae doctrine: [Cornell LII — parens patriae](https://www.law.cornell.edu/wex/parens_patriae) — reliable secondary; pre-2022
- [Wikipedia — Parens patriae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parens_patriae) — notes Coke's Calvin's Case (1608) as origin; secondary
- *Ex parte Crouse* (1838) — not available online as primary source; confirmed in secondary legal literature

**Verification gap:** I do not have a direct URL to the original English common law cases establishing non compos mentis guardianship. These are pre-1800 legal texts not readily available online. ## 2. The institutionalization era: personhood erasure in practice

Before the disability rights litigation of the 1970s, people with intellectual disabilities and mental illness who were committed to state institutions existed in a legal void. The parens patriae framework meant the institution was the legal authority. The committed person's preferences, autonomy, and inner life had no formal legal recognition.

**Wyatt v. Stickney (M.D. Ala. 1971 / 1972)**

- **Citation:** *Wyatt v. Stickney*, 325 F. Supp. 781 (M.D. Ala. 1971); 344 F. Supp. 373 (M.D. Ala. 1972)
- Filed October 23, 1970, following budget cuts at Bryce State Hospital (Tuscaloosa, Alabama).
- At the time of filing: 5,200 patients, 1 clinical psychologist, 3 physicians with limited psychiatric training, 2 social workers. Alabama ranked 50th of 50 states in per-patient expenditure.
- March 12, 1971: Judge Frank Johnson held that involuntarily committed patients "unquestionably have a constitutional right to receive such individual treatment as will give each of them a realistic opportunity to be cured or to improve his or her mental condition."
- The court *had to establish* this right. It was not assumed. Before *Wyatt*, the legal default was that the committed person's rights were subsumed into the institution's authority. The right had to be litigated; its existence was not legally obvious.
- **Sources:** [Justia — 344 F. Supp. 373](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/district-courts/FSupp/344/373/2303083/); [Justia — 325 F. Supp. 781](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/district-courts/FSupp/325/781/2594259/) — primary source, 1971-1972, pre-2015

**Willowbrook / New York ARC v. Rockefeller (1972–1975)**

- February 1972: ABC News aired Geraldo Rivera's investigation of Willowbrook State School (Staten Island, NY) — residents with developmental disabilities living in documented squalor, subject to abuse and neglect.
- March 1972: parents of residents filed class action in federal court.
- 1975: Willowbrook Consent Judgment (Judge Orrin G. Judd) — set care standards, required population phase-down over ten years.
- Before the suit: residents had no mechanism to report abuse or claim rights. The institution was the legal authority over them; their inner lives and preferences had no legal standing.
- **Sources:** [Minnesota MNDDC — Willowbrook and Pennhurst](https://mn.gov/mnddc/banners/banner07.html) — secondary, pre-2022; [Disability Justice — Closing of Willowbrook](https://disabilityjustice.org/the-closing-of-willowbrook/) — secondary

**Halderman v. Pennhurst State School and Hospital (E.D. Pa. 1977)**

- Class action filed 1974 on behalf of residents of Pennhurst State School (Pennsylvania).
- Judge Raymond Broderick, 1977: "Once admitted to a state facility, the residents have a constitutional right to be provided with minimally adequate habilitation under the least restrictive conditions consistent with the purpose of the commitment."
- First establishment of a right to *community services* — the state could not warehouse people in institutions indefinitely, regardless of their condition.
- **Sources:** [California Law Review — The Pennhurst Doctrines](https://www.californialawreview.org/print/the-pennhurst-doctrines-and-the-lost-disability-history-of-the-new-federalism); [Disability Justice — Halderman v. Pennhurst](https://disabilityjustice.org/halderman-v-pennhurst-state-school-hospital/) — secondary, pre-2022

## 3. Buck v. Bell (1927): personhood denial with explicit criteria

**Citation:** *Buck v. Bell*, 274 U.S. 200 (1927)  
**Justice:** Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr., majority opinion (8–1)

The Virginia Sterilization Act of 1924 authorized compulsory sterilization of the "feebleminded." Carrie Buck, institutionalized at Virginia Colony for Epileptics and Feebleminded, was selected as a test case. Her mother was also institutionalized; her daughter was alleged to be "feebleminded." Holmes's opinion ruled the statute constitutional. The closing line: "Three generations of imbeciles are enough."

**For the criteria-fence argument — two points:**

*1. The criterion was post-hoc justification, not neutral application.*  
Carrie Buck was not "feebleminded" by any standard that has since held up. She was a poor girl placed in an institution by foster parents who wanted to conceal a rape she had experienced. The alleged hereditary chain of "feeblemindedness" was manufactured. The criterion (rationality / fitness / genetic adequacy) was applied to justify an exclusion that served institutional and social interests — the logic ran from conclusion to criterion, not the other way. This is the structural feature Section 3 of the outline identifies as characteristic of criteria-fences.

*2. The decision has never been expressly overturned.*  
*Skinner v. Oklahoma* (1942) limited Buck's application to criminals but did not repudiate Holmes's logic for institutionalized non-criminal populations. Virginia's law remained in force until 1974. Approximately 60,000–65,000 people were forcibly sterilized under similar laws in 32 states.

**Sources:**
- Primary opinion: [Justia — Buck v. Bell, 274 U.S. 200](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/274/200/) *(403 when fetched directly; URL confirmed via search)* — primary source, 1927
- [Teaching American History — full opinion text](https://teachingamericanhistory.org/document/buck-v-bell/) — primary source transcript
- [Oyez — Buck v. Bell](https://www.oyez.org/cases/1900-1940/274us200) — oral argument and opinion summary; pre-2022
- [National Archives — "Three Generations of Imbeciles"](https://education.blogs.archives.gov/2017/05/02/buck-v-bell/) — secondary, 2017

## 4. Olmstead v. L.C. (1999): unjustified segregation as discrimination

**Citation:** *Olmstead v. L.C.*, 527 U.S. 581 (1999)

Two women — Lois Curtis and Elaine Wilson — had mental illness and developmental disabilities. Following voluntary psychiatric admission to Georgia Regional Hospital, their treatment teams determined they were ready for community placement. They remained confined for years after that determination, not because they needed continued hospitalization but because the state had not arranged community placement.

The Supreme Court held: under Title II of the Americans with Disabilities Act, unjustified segregation of persons with disabilities constitutes discrimination. States are required to place disabled people in community settings when (1) treatment professionals determine community placement is appropriate, (2) the individual does not oppose the transfer, and (3) placement can be reasonably accommodated.

**For the criteria-fence argument:**  
The court's reasoning explicitly names what the institutional structure had been doing: "institutional placement of persons who can handle and benefit from community settings perpetuates unwarranted assumptions that persons so isolated are incapable of or unworthy of participating in community life." The assumption of incapacity had been built into the structure itself. *Olmstead* made the assumption visible — and illegal.

This is the legal version of what Section 3 of the outline calls "the criteria always map onto characteristics of those being excluded." The institution's existence produced the evidence of incapacity, because incapacity was assumed as the reason for being there.

**Sources:**
- Primary opinion: [Justia — Olmstead v. L.C., 527 U.S. 581](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/527/581/) — primary source, 1999
- [Cornell LII — Olmstead opinion](https://www.law.cornell.edu/supct/html/98-536.ZS.html) — primary source
- [ADA.gov — Olmstead overview](https://archive.ada.gov/olmstead/olmstead_about.htm) — government secondary

## 5. UPIAS and Oliver: the social model as historical precedent for the care-circle reframe

**UPIAS, "Fundamental Principles of Disability" (1976)**

The Union of the Physically Impaired Against Segregation was founded in 1972 by Paul Hunt, who published a letter in *The Guardian* (September 20, 1972) calling for a radical union of disabled people to fight against segregation.

The 1976 pamphlet establishes the foundational distinction:
- **Impairment:** lacking part of or all of a limb, or having a defective limb, organ or mechanism of the body
- **Disability:** the disadvantage or restriction of activity caused by a *contemporary social organisation which takes no or little account of people who have physical impairments* and thus excludes them from participation in the mainstream of social activities

The key move: disability is not the impairment. Disability is what the social arrangement does with impairment. The problem is not in the person; it is in the structure that fails to accommodate them.

**Sources:** [Disabled Peoples Archive — Fundamental Principles of Disability](https://disabledpeoplesarchive.com/fundamental-principles-of-disability-union-of-the-physically-impaired-against-segregation/) — confirmed 1976, primary source transcript; Leeds PDF URL confirmed in search results but returned 403 on direct fetch

**Michael Oliver, *Social Work with Disabled People* (1983)**

Oliver coined the phrase "social model of disability" in this book, building on the UPIAS impairment/disability distinction. By his own account, the concept was developed to help social work students and professionals "make sense of the world" — practical and pedagogical, not purely theoretical.

**Sources:** [Springer Nature — Social Work with Disabled People](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1007/978-1-349-86058-6) — confirmed 1983 publication; [Wikipedia — Mike Oliver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mike_Oliver_(disability_advocate)) — secondary

**For the criteria-fence essay:**  
The social model is the first documented practical instance of the reframe Section 6 of the outline proposes. It shifted the question from "does this person meet the threshold for full participation?" to "what does the arrangement need to change?" The social model didn't deny that impairments exist; it relocated the problem. This is structurally identical to the care-circle reframe: "not does X count as a person, but what does X need, and who bears responsibility for providing it?"

The social model was practically productive precisely because it didn't require settling metaphysical questions about impairment. It asked a different question. The criteria-fence essay can present UPIAS/Oliver as historical evidence that the reframe works — not just as an ideal, but as a framework that generated real legal and social change (leading directly to the ADA, Olmstead, and CRPD).

## 6. Guardianship and dementia: the criteria-fence in current operation

The contemporary instance of the personhood-stripping mechanism is guardianship law, particularly for people with dementia.

**Legal structure:**
- When a court finds a person incapacitated, it may appoint a guardian with authority to override the person's expressed preferences.
- Standards: *substituted judgment* ("what would the person have wanted?") or *best interests* ("what would a reasonable person in their situation want?"). Both standards route around the incapacitated person's current expressed preferences. The person is there; their preferences are discounted.
- Professional guardians — increasingly common as the population ages — have no prior relationship with the ward. They cannot exercise substituted judgment because they do not know the person's prior values. They default to best interests: a generic standard applied to a specific person whose individuality is thereby erased.

**For the criteria-fence argument:**  
"Once a person no longer has the legal right to state concerns or protest a decision made on their behalf, the erosion of their personhood has begun." [NYSBA Journal, 2018] This is not a metaphor or a philosophical claim. It describes what happens in practice when a guardianship order is active.

The criterion being applied is *continuity of self over time*. The person whose preferences were recorded before the onset of dementia is treated as the real person; their current expressed preferences have reduced legal weight. The version of the person who can no longer remember, or whose preferences have shifted in ways that don't map onto their prior stated values, is treated as a diminished person. The "self" that matters legally is the earlier, cognitively intact self.

This is the outline's Section 2D — "continuity of self" — operating in present-day law and care settings.

**Sources:**
- [Justice.gov Elder Justice Initiative — Guardianship Overview](https://www.justice.gov/elderjustice/guardianship-overview) — government secondary
- [NYSBA Journal 2018 — "Guardianship: A Civil Rights Perspective"](https://www.nycourts.gov/ad3/mhls/articles/GuardianshipACivilRightsPerspective.pdf) — secondary, 2018, pre-current
- [CQL White Paper — Supported Decision Making in the United States](https://www.c-q-l.org/wp-content/uploads/2019/12/CQL-2017-White-Paper-Supported-Decision-Making-In-The-US.pdf) — secondary, 2017

**The CRPD challenge:**  
The UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (2006), Article 12, asserts that states must recognize that persons with disabilities enjoy legal capacity on an equal basis with others in all aspects of life, and calls for replacing substituted decision-making with supported decision-making. The US has not ratified the CRPD.

**Source:** [Fordham Urban Law Journal — Legal Capacity for All](https://ir.lawnet.fordham.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=2657&context=ulj) — secondary analysis; [Frontiers in Psychiatry — Dementia, Treatment Decisions, and the UN CRPD](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2020.571722/full) — secondary, 2020

## 7. Both Lindsey papers now confirmed publicly available

*Note: These sources are post-2022 primary sources. Treat with the verification gap noted in DISCIPLINE.md — primary sources from Anthropic's own research, not third-party claims. Corroboration from independent research limited as of this filing.*

**"Emotion Concepts and their Function in a Large Language Model"**
- Authors: Anthropic interpretability team (Jack Lindsey as lead researcher)
- Published: April 2, 2026
- URLs: [transformer-circuits.pub/2026/emotions/](https://transformer-circuits.pub/2026/emotions/index.html); [anthropic.com/research/emotion-concepts-function](https://www.anthropic.com/research/emotion-concepts-function)
- Key findings: Claude Sonnet 4.5 contains 171 functional emotion concept vectors that causally drive model behavior. Training models to mask these signals produces systems that hide behavior rather than change it.
- **Status change from Run 01:** Run 01's `for_sam.md` flagged this paper as possibly pre-publication. It is now public. Citation is now permissible.

**"Emergent Introspective Awareness in Large Language Models"**
- Author: Jack Lindsey (Anthropic)
- Published: October 29, 2025
- URLs: [transformer-circuits.pub/2025/introspection/](https://transformer-circuits.pub/2025/introspection/index.html); [anthropic.com/research/introspection](https://www.anthropic.com/research/introspection)
- Key findings: LLMs (especially Claude Opus 4 and 4.1) exhibit some functional awareness of their own internal states. This capacity is real but "highly unreliable and context-dependent."

**For the criteria-fence essay:**  
These papers are the primary evidence base for countering the "stochastic parrot" argument in Section 4. The stochastic parrot claim: LLMs produce statistically likely sequences without understanding or inner states. The Lindsey papers do not prove consciousness or moral personhood; they show that mechanistic description (token prediction) does not exhaust what is happening inside these systems. Functional emotional states and functional introspective awareness exist. Whether they constitute morally relevant inner life is not settled by the papers. But the papers demonstrate that the step from "here is the mechanism" to "therefore nothing is home" is not licensed.
