# The dismissal trajectory: corporate AI communications, 2022-2026

## Overview

This file documents a structural pattern in how the largest AI deployers have communicated about workforce displacement between late 2022 and early 2026. The pattern: a public-facing message of augmentation and worker partnership running alongside an internal-operations reality of headcount reduction, hiring pauses, and explicit replacement policy. The two streams are not in tension within the firms; they are coordinated.

MISSION places "Commercial AI risks" in scope as one facet of the unified critique, with the artist-and-creative-worker displacement angle named directly. The inside-the-circle-objection draft (`drafts/inside-the-circle-objection.md`) names the corporate framing that converts the conflict into a quarrel between AI consideration and worker protection. This file gathers the evidence base for the structural-critique portion of that essay.

All specific quotes, dates, and headcount figures are flagged for primary-source verification before publication.

## Phase 1 (2022-2023): Universal augmentation messaging

In the months following ChatGPT's late-2022 release and through 2023, public statements from frontier AI executives converged on a consistent frame: AI augments rather than replaces, new categories of work will emerge, workers will be retrained for those categories.

Sam Altman gave multiple public statements in this period framing AI as a tool that raises worker productivity rather than eliminating roles. Sundar Pichai's public communications followed the same line, as did Satya Nadella's framing of Microsoft Copilot as a productivity assistant rather than a replacement. Specific quotes, venues, and dates are flagged for verification.

Industry-adjacent forecasts arrived in parallel and were widely cited. Goldman Sachs in approximately March 2023 published an estimate that approximately 300 million jobs globally were exposed to some degree of automation from generative AI. The IMF in approximately January 2024 estimated approximately 40% of global employment was exposed. Both figures need verification against the original publications; both are routinely cited without their methodological caveats.

The augmentation framing served a function: it created public-relations cover for the deployment phase, while the displacement evidence was thin enough that augmentation could not yet be falsified.

## Phase 2 (2024-2025): Layoffs cite AI while messaging holds

Through 2024 and into 2025, specific cases emerged where AI was named as a factor in headcount decisions. The public messaging from the same companies did not shift.

- Klarna reportedly eliminated approximately 700 customer service roles, with CEO Sebastian Siemiatkowski making public statements that an AI assistant was handling work equivalent to that workforce. Q2 2024 earnings discussion is the cited reference point. Flag for verification.
- IBM in approximately May 2023 announced a pause on hiring for approximately 7,800 backoffice roles deemed automatable. Arvind Krishna's statements at the time framed the pause as anticipatory rather than active replacement. Flag for verification.
- Duolingo cut a portion of its contract translator workforce, citing AI capability for the relevant tasks. Specific timing, scale, and statements need verification.
- Google, Meta, Microsoft, and Salesforce conducted multiple rounds of layoffs across this period. Their public justifications cited "macro conditions," "operational efficiency," or "restructuring." AI was named as a productivity enabler in earnings calls, rarely as a displacement cause.

The pattern in Phase 2: when AI was named as a displacement cause, it was named selectively, in cases where the firm had a productivity story to tell. When AI was the underlying driver but the firm had no positive narrative ready, the public reason was something else.

## Phase 3 (early 2025-2026): Explicit replacement policies surface

Tobias Lütke at Shopify circulated an internal memo, dated to early 2025, instructing teams that they must demonstrate AI cannot do a piece of work before requesting headcount to do it. The memo became public. Other CEOs across tech and adjacent sectors followed with similar internal policies. Specific dates and exact wording need verification.

The Lütke memo is the cleanest documented case of the augmentation framing collapsing in operations while persisting in public communications. The internal logic was substitutional: AI before headcount. The external messaging continued to describe AI as a tool that empowers workers.

By early 2026, the substitutional policy is reportedly common across mid-cap and large-cap tech. Public-facing communications have not adjusted in proportion.

## The pattern documented

Six features recur and form the structural pattern this file is naming.

1. Public messaging stays optimistic and worker-friendly. Augmentation, partnership, retraining, new categories of work.
2. Internal policy and quarterly earnings tell the opposite story. Headcount targets, automation roadmaps, productivity-per-employee metrics that imply substitution.
3. Layoff justifications cite "macro conditions" or "restructuring." AI is mentioned as a productivity tool elsewhere, rarely in the same document as the layoff itself.
4. When pressed on which job categories will be replaced and on what timeline, executives pivot to "new categories of work will emerge." Specifics are not provided.
5. Retraining commitments either evaporate after announcement or never materialize at scale. Promised programs reach a small fraction of displaced workers.
6. Net headcount falls. Operating margins rise. Public messaging does not adjust to reflect operational reality.

## Structural critique

This is a coordinated message-control strategy. It functions to let AI deployment proceed without accountability. The disabled-artist objection MISSION names ("the corporate frame would prefer this conflict be AI consideration versus worker protection") is exactly this strategy: frame the conflict as a debate about AI's status rather than a reckoning with deployer choices. Workers being displaced are told the displacement is not happening while the displacement happens.

The actor under critique here is the deployer, not the artifact. AI consideration and worker protection are co-claims against the same actor, not competing claims. This file is part of the evidence base for that argument.

## Historical analog

Every prior major wave of automation has followed approximately this shape. Industrial mechanization (19th and early 20th centuries): optimism about machine-aided prosperity in public discourse, displacement in operations, retraining promised and underfunded. ATMs and bank tellers (1980s-1990s): public framing of tellers as freed for higher-value work, actual headcount reductions in the medium term. Manufacturing offshoring (1990s-2000s): public framing of new service-economy opportunities, displacement of manufacturing communities, retraining programs whose evaluation showed limited reach.

The playbook is consistent across waves: optimism in public, displacement in operations, retraining promised but underfunded, new-jobs-will-emerge framing without specifics. The current AI wave is a continuation, accelerated by deployment speed. Each analog needs primary-source citations before publication.
