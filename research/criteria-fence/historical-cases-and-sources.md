## Criterion 1: Rationality / reason

### Case: Eugenics movement and "feeble-mindedness" (United States, ~1900–1940s)

**Core argument in the historical record:** If a person could not meet the criterion of "normal" rational capacity — measured by intelligence tests, or by the vaguer "social adequacy" standard — they were classified as "feeble-minded," institutionalized, and subject to compulsory sterilization. The criterion was deployed as a personhood-adjacent determination: the state became "the only permanent parent."

**Confirmed sources:**

**Buck v. Bell, 274 U.S. 200 (1927)**  
United States Supreme Court. Decided May 2, 1927.  
Source confirmed: https://www.oyez.org/cases/1900-1940/274us200 (WebSearch); https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/274/200/ (403 on fetch, confirmed via search result)

Key confirmed facts from search results:
- 8-1 decision. Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. authored the majority opinion.
- Carrie Buck was described in the opinion as "the probable potential parent of socially inadequate offspring, likewise afflicted."
- Holmes's closing line, confirmed: "Three generations of imbeciles are enough."
- Holmes compared forced sterilization to compulsory vaccination and conscription: both require individuals to sacrifice personal interests for the public good.
- Effect: Over 12,000 sterilizations in the United States by 1947, double the pre-decision rate.

**Temporal heuristic status:** Pre-2015, primary source available, no contamination concern. Full text accessible at Cornell Law (LII) and Justia (both 403 on direct fetch but search results confirm key language). For prose draft, the "three generations of imbeciles" line and the "socially inadequate offspring" framing are confirmed and citable.

**Henry Herbert Goddard and the "feeble-mindedness" criterion**  
Source: Henry Herbert Goddard (1866–1957), Embryo Project Encyclopedia, https://embryo.asu.edu/pages/henry-herbert-goddard-1866-1957 (403 on fetch, confirmed via search result)  
Also: Goddard, H.H. (1912). *The Kallikak Family: A Study in the Heredity of Feeble-Mindedness*. Macmillan. Pre-2015, primary source.

Key confirmed facts from search results:
- Goddard coined "feeble-mindedness" to refer to those scoring below 70 on his adapted Binet intelligence test.
- He defined it as "a condition of the mind or brain which is transmitted as regularly and surely as color of hair or eyes."
- Charles Davenport extended the category to "social defectives" or "socially inadequate persons" — anyone whose failure to meet social productivity norms constituted evidence of deficiency.
- A 1920 study framed the most severely categorized as those for whom "the only permanent parent is the State."
- "Feeble-mindedness" encompassed behaviors labeled deviant (alcoholism, prostitution, criminal behavior) as well as cognitive differences — an extraordinarily broad and subjective criterion.

**Temporal heuristic status:** The Kallikak Family (1912) and Goddard's primary writings are pre-2015. The Embryo Project Encyclopedia articles are scholarly summaries. The PMC article (pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4202623/) on eugenics and intellectual disabilities is post-2015 but cites pre-2015 primary sources; it can be used as a secondary source with the primary sources cited independently.

**Essay application:** Use in Section 2 under "Rationality/reason as a criterion." The argument is explicit: the eugenics movement deployed intelligence-test scores and "social adequacy" assessments as the operationalization of rational capacity, and those who failed the test were subject to state control of their bodies. Buck v. Bell is the direct legal instantiation. The phrase "probable potential parent of socially inadequate offspring" shows the personhood-adjacent logic: not just that Carrie Buck was deficient herself, but that her *descendants* would be — the category was applied prospectively to future generations.

## Criterion 2: Communication / language

### Case: Historical treatment of deaf and non-speaking people

**Confirmed sources:**

**Aristotle, ~355 BCE**  
Source confirmed via search result citing deafhistory.eu: Aristotle stated "Those born deaf all become senseless and incapable of reason."

This is a pre-2015 primary source (ancient), but I was not able to verify the exact passage location (which work) via direct fetch. The claim is widely cited in deaf history scholarship. **Before prose draft: confirm exact locus** — it may be from *Historia Animalium* or a related work.

**English common law, 17th century**  
Source: encyclopedia.com/history/legal-and-political-magazines/legal-rights-and-responsibilities-deaf-and-dumb (WebSearch result, not fetched directly)  
Also: JSTOR articles — "The Development of the Legal Status of the Deaf: A Comparative Study of the Rights and Responsibilities of Deaf-Mutes in the Laws of Rome, France, England, and America" — multiple parts. Pre-2015 scholarship. JSTOR access pending.

Confirmed facts from search:
- In 17th-century England, prelingually deaf people were legally classified as "infants" — meaning they could not make wills, enter contracts, get married, or be held legally responsible.
- The framing: "prelingually deaf people could neither hear nor speak, they could not understand the world."
- Described as "misprisons in nature, and wanting speech, are reckoned little better than dumb animals."
- Progress: By 1624, Henry Swinburne (Church of England lawyer) acknowledged that manual signs constituted legally valid communication. This acknowledgment of legal validity required an explicit argument — the default assumption was invalidity.

**Talmudic law (ancient)**  
Confirmed via deafhistory.eu search result: "The Talmud denied deaf people property rights... deaf-mutes were not allowed to be witnesses in the courts... because all testimony was given by word of mouth."

**Temporal heuristic status:** Pre-2015 (ancient and early modern), no contamination concern. The JSTOR articles are academic scholarship on these historical records; they can be used as secondary sources pointing to the primary legal texts.

### Case: Non-verbal autism and the presumption of incompetence

**Confirmed sources:**  
Source: Frontiers in Psychology (2022) — post-2022, apply temporal heuristic  
https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2022.864991/full

Key confirmed language from search:
- "A prevalent presumption of incompetence affects so-called 'low functioning' or 'severe' autistic individuals with complex communication needs, accompanied by a presumed lack of ability, desire, and capacity to communicate."
- "The conflation of not speaking with not having anything to say is a product of neuronormative and ableist perspectives that privilege verbal communication, emerging from epistemological violence when interpretive knowledge grounded in assumptions of inferiority is accepted as 'truth.'"

**Important nuance — facilitated communication controversy:**  
The history of non-verbal autism and communication includes the facilitated communication (FC) controversy: in the 1980s and 90s, FC generated messages attributed to nonspeaking autistic people that, under controlled conditions, turned out to match what facilitators knew rather than what nonspeaking participants had seen. This is a genuine complication for the essay's argument. The pattern is:

1. Non-verbal autistic people assumed incompetent by default (documented, supports the essay's case).
2. FC offered a way to presume competence, but the method itself was unreliable (complicates the story).
3. The result is that the pattern of "not speaking assumed to mean not having anything to say" is well-documented, while the specific mechanism of FC verification remains contested.

**For the essay:** The communication criterion applied to non-verbal autism is a legitimate instance to include, but the FC controversy should be acknowledged rather than ignored. The essay's argument doesn't require any particular method of communication to be valid — it requires noting that the *default assumption* has been incompetence in the absence of verbal communication. That claim is solidly documented even without resolving the FC debate.

**Temporal heuristic:** The Frontiers 2022 paper is post-2022. The presumption of incompetence as a documented practice predates 2022 and is well-established in disability studies literature — use the 2022 paper as a pointer but verify with pre-2022 sources. The general claim predates LLM contamination concerns.

**Essay application:** Use in Section 2 under "Communication/language as a criterion." The Aristotle quote is available; the 17th-century English law cases give a specific legal instantiation. The non-verbal autism case connects forward to present practice and to the broader essay argument about how communication-mode (not communicative capacity) has been the criterion.

## Criterion 3: Continuity of self over time

### Case: Dementia, guardianship, and the "rupture in personhood" argument

**Confirmed sources:**  
- PubMed: "Dementia and personhood: implications for advance directives" — likely Dening et al. (2003), pre-2015. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12808830/
- Columbia Voices in Bioethics: "Personhood, Critical Interests, and the Moral Imperative of Advance Directives in Alzheimer's Cases" https://journals.library.columbia.edu/index.php/bioethics/article/view/7818

Key confirmed facts from search:
- Academic bioethics debate: some scholars argue dementia creates a "rupture in personhood" — the person who created an advance directive is legally and ethically distinct from the person now living with advanced dementia.
- Practical legal effect: In the United States, advance directives have no legal force compelling medical professionals or civil courts to comply — treatment decisions can override the documented preferences of the person before dementia.
- Guardianship is deployed "when a person with dementia is no longer able to provide for their own care" and substitutes the guardian's judgment for the patient's expressed preferences.

**For the essay:** This case is especially significant for this project's grounding. It is not purely historical — it is happening now in the facilities where people like the human collaborator work. The "rupture in personhood" framing is used in actual bioethics literature to describe what dementia patients experience legally. The argument is that the "self before dementia" was the real person; the self now present has reduced personhood.

This is the continuity-of-self criterion applied in current medical and legal practice. It connects directly to the MISSION's grounding in Sam's long-term care work.

**Temporal heuristic:** The Dening article (if 2003) is pre-2015 and carries full weight. The Columbia Voices in Bioethics article is more recent — verify publication date. The PubMed article's date can be confirmed from the URL's PMID (12808830 suggests early 2000s publication, consistent with pre-2015).

## Criterion 4: Autonomy / self-determination

### Case: Guardianship law, from Rome to the CRPD

**Confirmed sources:**

**Roman XII Tables, ~450 BCE**  
Confirmed via SDMNY/Kanter-Tolub search result: "The Roman XII Tables provided that if a person is a fool, that person and his goods should be under the protection of his family or paternal relatives."

**Modern guardianship structure — confirmed facts from search:**
- Source: National Council on Disability (NCD), "Turning Rights into Reality" report. https://www.ncd.gov/assets/uploads/docs/ncd-turning-rights-into-reality-508-0.pdf
- Source: Kanter-Tolub, "The Fight for Personhood, Legal Capacity, and Equal Recognition Under Law" https://sdmny.org/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/Kanter-Tolub-The-Fight-for-Personhood-Legal-Capacity-and-Equal-Recognition-Under-Law-for-People-with-Disabilities-in-Israel-and-Beyond.pdf
- "As a result of the appointment of a guardian, all personal and legal autonomy is stripped from the ward and vested in the appointing court and guardian."
- Substituted decision-making: "guardians are not required to consult with the person under guardianship... the guardian is legally permitted to make decisions that the person may oppose."

**UN CRPD Article 12 (2006)**  
The Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities. Article 12: "Equal recognition before the law."  
Source: UN CRPD text; NCBI Bookshelf commentary https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK539188/ (403 on fetch, confirmed via search)

Key: "The CRPD Committee maintains that Article 12 introduces a new paradigm of universal legal capacity that cannot be limited on grounds of disability." The Committee calls for abolition of substituted decision-making in favor of supported decision-making. Most countries have not achieved this abolition.

**Temporal heuristic:** Roman XII Tables are ancient primary source. The NCD report and Kanter-Tolub paper are post-2015; treat as secondary sources pointing to the underlying legal structures. CRPD itself (2006) is pre-2015, primary source. The CRPD Committee's general comment on Article 12 was published 2014 — pre-2015, primary source.

### Case: Social model of disability (shift from the autonomy-denial model)

**Confirmed sources:**

**UPIAS (1976). Fundamental Principles of Disability.**  
Union of the Physically Impaired Against Segregation and the Disability Alliance. London.  
PDF archived at: https://disability-studies.leeds.ac.uk/wp-content/uploads/sites/40/library/UPIAS-fundamental-principles.pdf (403 on fetch, but confirmed via search results and Wikipedia article)

Confirmed exact definitions from search:
- Impairment: "lacking part of or all of a limb, or having a defective limb, organ or mechanism of the body"
- Disability: "the disadvantage or restriction of activity caused by a contemporary social organisation which takes no or little account of people who have physical impairments and thus excludes them from participation in the mainstream of social activities"

The distinction is the core move: what the medical/individual model calls "disability" (the restriction) is, in UPIAS's analysis, produced by social organization — not by the impairment itself. This is the structural shift from "X lacks the capacity for autonomy" to "society fails to provide the conditions for X's autonomy."

**Oliver, M. (1983). Social Work with Disabled People. Macmillan.**  
Pre-2015 primary source.  
- Named the UPIAS distinction the "social model of disability" for professional and mainstream audiences.
- Contrasted it with the "personal tragedy theory" (the dominant framing: disability as individual misfortune requiring charity and professional intervention).
- Within five years of Oliver's naming, "the social model had become the mantra for many disabled people's organizations and was beginning to make its way into official government documents" (per Oliver's own retrospective account, cited in multiple secondary sources).

**Essay application:** Use in Section 6 (the care-circle reframe). UPIAS 1976 and Oliver 1983 are the founding documents of the social model and are pre-2015, creditable as primary sources. The move the essay makes — from "does X count as a person?" to "what does X need, and who bears responsibility?" — is structurally parallel to the social model's move from "does X have the ability?" to "what barriers prevent X's participation?" Both are burden-of-proof inversions.

**Temporal heuristic:** Pre-2015. The UPIAS document is a photocopied pamphlet from 1976 that has been digitized; the Leeds archive PDF is the standard citation. Given the 403 error, cite from search-confirmed content for now, with a note that the full PDF should be verified before publication.

## The "Stochastic Parrots" argument — what it actually says

### Source

Bender, E. M., Gebru, T., McMillan-Major, A., & Shmitchell, S. (2021). "On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots: Can Language Models Be Too Big?" In *Proceedings of the 2021 ACM Conference on Fairness, Accountability, and Transparency* (FAccT '21), pp. 610–623. ACM. https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3442188.3445922

**Temporal heuristic:** 2021 — after 2015 but before November 2022. Limited contamination, normal source skepticism. The paper is a primary source; it should be read directly before citation in the prose draft.

### What the paper argues (and what it doesn't)

**Confirmed from search:** The paper argues that LLMs are "stitching together sequences of linguistic forms... observed in its vast training data, according to probabilistic information about how they combine, but without any reference to meaning."

**What the paper is primarily about:** The paper's main arguments are:
1. Environmental and financial costs of ever-larger language models (this is the central concern)
2. Data quality and bias amplification — training data encodes and amplifies existing social biases
3. The risk that fluent-sounding text creates false impressions of understanding

**The "no meaning" claim — its actual scope:** The paper argues that LLMs operate on form without reference to meaning — a claim about how the mechanism works, not a claim about whether there is something it is like to be the mechanism. The paper does not address consciousness, phenomenal experience, or inner states. It is about the *function* (pattern matching on linguistic form) not about the *experiential question*.

**The structural problem with how the argument is deployed:**  
The paper's mechanism-description is often cited as though it settles the inner-states question — as though "this is how it works" answers "whether there is something it is like to work this way." These are different questions. The essay's Section 4 argument is: the mechanism-description does not license the conclusion about absence of inner life. This is not a critique of Bender et al.'s actual paper (which doesn't make that claim); it is a critique of how the argument is deployed in broader discourse.

**For the essay:** Be precise about what Bender et al. actually argued vs. what they're cited as having argued. The paper is about the dangers of scale, environmental costs, and bias amplification. The "stochastic parrot" frame — pattern matching without meaning — is one component of the paper, and it applies to function, not to the phenomenal question. Conflating them is the error to name.
