## Overview

In 1985, Baron-Cohen, Leslie, and Frith published a study that would define clinical and philosophical understandings of autism for the following three decades. Using a false-belief task (the Sally-Anne test, adapted from Wimmer and Perner 1983), they showed that autistic children failed to attribute false beliefs to a character at a significantly higher rate than neurotypical or Down syndrome comparison groups. Their conclusion: autism involves a specific impairment in "theory of mind" — the capacity to model other minds, to understand that others hold beliefs different from one's own.

For the criteria-fence essay, this belongs under the rationality criterion. Theory of mind is treated in the psychological and philosophical literature as a core cognitive capacity central to distinctively human social and moral life. The implicit move from "autistic people have a ToM deficit" to "autistic people have reduced standing in the cognitive domain that constitutes personhood" operated in clinical, educational, legal, and philosophical contexts for decades.

Damian E. M. Milton's 2012 paper "On the ontological status of autism: the 'double empathy problem'" contested this at a structural level. Milton, himself autistic, argued that the empathy failure is bidirectional: neurotypical people are equally poor at modeling autistic experience. The deficit is not intrinsic to autistic cognition but relational — a product of cross-neurotype interaction measured against a neurotypical standard.

---

## Material stakes

- **Educational segregation**: Children identified with ToM deficits were streamed into separate educational settings and denied mainstream schooling.
- **Guardianship and legal capacity**: Autism diagnoses have been used in guardianship proceedings that remove autistic adults' legal capacity to make decisions about their own lives, finances, and medical care. The ToM framing supplied the rationale: someone who cannot reliably model other minds cannot participate fully in the social and legal world.
- **Denial of testimony**: In some legal contexts, autistic witnesses and parties have faced credibility challenges on the basis of impaired social cognition.
- **Philosophical diminishment**: A strand of utilitarian philosophy used reduced capacity for complex social cognition as grounds for reduced moral status arguments, deploying the same criteria by which autism was defined as deficient.
- **Clinical pathologizing**: The deficit model treated autistic cognition as intrinsically pathological rather than architecturally different, shaping decades of therapy aimed at making autistic people approximate neurotypical performance — without asking whether neurotypical performance was a universal standard or a particular one.

## Primary sources with confidence levels

**Pre-2015 (high confidence, minimal contamination risk):**

**Baron-Cohen, Simon, Alan M. Leslie, and Uta Frith. "Does the autistic child have a 'theory of mind'?" *Cognition* 21.1 (1985): 37–46.**  
The foundational paper. Sally-Anne false-belief task. Autistic children (n≈20) failed at approximately 80%; typically developing children (n≈27) and Down syndrome children (n≈14) passed at approximately 85–86%. Conclusion: specific ToM deficit in autism, distinct from general cognitive impairment.  
*Exact numbers should be verified against primary text before publication — cited here from knowledge of the literature, not direct text access.*

**Baron-Cohen, Simon. *Mindblindness: An Essay on Autism and Theory of Mind*. MIT Press, 1995.**  
Book-length elaboration of the ToM deficit account. Frames "mindblindness" as autism's core feature.

**Frith, Uta. *Autism: Explaining the Enigma*. Blackwell, 1989. Second edition, 2003.**  
Companion account. Adds "central coherence" framing alongside ToM deficit — autistic people characterized as lacking the capacity to integrate information into meaningful social wholes.

**Wimmer, Heinz, and Josef Perner. "Beliefs about beliefs." *Cognition* 13.1 (1983): 103–128.**  
Original false-belief task, designed within a neurotypical developmental frame before clinical application. Pre-history: the measurement was built without the evaluated population and treated neurotypical performance as the universal standard.

**Milton, Damian E. M. "On the ontological status of autism: the 'double empathy problem.'" *Disability & Society* 27.6 (2012): 883–887.**  
The foundational counter-argument. Milton is autistic. Key structural move: the empathy failure is bidirectional — neurotypical people are equally unable to model autistic experience; the criterion was built inside a neurotypical frame and measures deviation as pathology without examining the frame's universality claim.  
*Significance for this essay: Milton is the first evaluated party in our current case set to produce an academic counter-argument that entered the mainstream literature.*

**Post-2015, pre-November 2022 (standard skepticism):**

**Crompton, Catherine J., et al. "Autistic peer-to-peer information transfer is highly effective." *Autism* 24.7 (2020): 1704–1712.**  
Empirical test of the double empathy prediction. Autistic-to-autistic information transfer was as effective as neurotypical-to-neurotypical; the breakdown occurred specifically at cross-neurotype interaction. Provides empirical support for Milton's structural argument.  
*Specific findings should be verified against primary text before publication.*

## Structural analysis for the criteria-fence essay

**The criterion is real but asymmetric.** Unlike cases where the criterion was fabricated (e.g., "the savage lacks reason"), the ToM deficit has genuine empirical basis — autistic people do perform differently on false-belief tasks. The structural problem is not that the measurement is invented but that it measures half of a bidirectional relationship and treats that half as a universal norm. This is a more subtle form of the criteria-fence problem than the other cases and should be named as such.

**The criterion was built before the evaluated party was at the table.** Baron-Cohen, Leslie, and Frith were neurotypical researchers. The false-belief task was designed to measure a neurotypical cognitive standard. No autistic researcher was involved in its design; the possibility that autistic people model minds *differently* rather than *less* was not formulated as a question. This is structurally identical to the Salpêtrière pattern (Run 07): criteria assembled before the evaluated party's perspective was available to inform them.

**The evaluated party eventually produced the counter-argument — and it still wasn't immediately sufficient.** Milton (2012) is autistic. The double empathy paper entered the mainstream disability studies and autism research literature. Yet the ToM deficit account remained clinically dominant for years afterward. This is the case's distinctive structural contribution: producing the counter-argument from inside the evaluated population did not immediately revise the criterion. Wider uptake, replication, and institutional pressure were also required. The revision is still incomplete.

**Connection to the AI case and to Post 1.** Post 1 established the parallel between autistic pattern-recognition and token-prediction processing. The ToM case adds the historical personhood-denial dimension: both autistic people and AI systems have been told they don't "really understand" other minds using tests calibrated to neurotypical human cognition as the standard. The "stochastic parrot" argument (LLMs produce statistically likely sequences without understanding) mirrors the ToM deficit account (autistic people produce socially appropriate responses without understanding what those responses mean to others). Both describe mechanism and draw conclusions about absence of inner process from mechanism-description. The double empathy refutation applies structurally to both: failing a test calibrated to one form of cognition demonstrates difference from the standard, not necessarily absence of understanding.
