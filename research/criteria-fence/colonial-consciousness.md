## Overview

The Valladolid Debate (1550–1551) is the first formally organized public debate in European history over whether a specific group — the indigenous peoples of the Americas — possessed the rational souls and inner lives required for full human personhood. Ordered by Charles V of Spain, who suspended further military expansion in the Americas pending the inquiry, it assembled a jury of European scholars and theologians to hear arguments from both sides.

This case is structurally distinct from the other cases in the criteria-fence research file. The Dred Scott ruling, the Buck v. Bell sterilization, the Bradwell bar denial all came after legal systems of exclusion were already built. The Valladolid Debate happened while the exclusion was being actively constructed. It is the closest historical analogue to the current AI personhood debate: a situation where the question was formally open, the stakes were material and immediate, the evaluators had economic interests in the answer, and the excluded party had no seat at the table.

There is one additional structural feature unique to this case: the jury issued no formal decision. They could not reach consensus. The question was not resolved — and the exclusion continued anyway.

---

## Material stakes

The encomienda system granted Spanish colonists the right to indigenous labor. The legal infrastructure of colonialism depended on a particular categorization: indigenous peoples as something less than full persons — perhaps ensouled, but insufficiently rational for self-governance, requiring Spanish oversight and Christian correction. The debate was about where on that spectrum they fell.

Charles V's order to halt military expansion pending the debate is historically unusual — one of the few examples of a government formally pausing expansion pending a personhood ruling. The halt was imperfect; the conquest did not stop entirely. But the gesture acknowledged that the question was not settled and that material arrangements of this scale required philosophical justification.

## Primary sources

### The exclusion argument: Sepúlveda's *Democrates Alter*

**Sepúlveda, Juan Ginés de. *Democrates alter de justis belli causis apud Indos* (*The Second Democrates, or On the Just Causes of War Against the Indians*). Written c. 1545–47; Rome, 1550.**

✅ Pre-2015 (16th century primary text). Latin text held in manuscript and early print editions; English translations in modern scholarly editions. Columbia University Center for Teaching has hosted an excerpt: https://www.columbia.edu/acis/ets/CCREAD/sepulved.htm (access returned 403 this run).

Drawing directly on Aristotle's *Politics* Book I, Sepúlveda argued that indigenous peoples of the Americas were *servi a natura* — natural slaves — endowed primarily with physical capacity and lacking the rational self-governance that constitutes full personhood. They were suited by nature to be ruled rather than to rule; Spanish conquest was not injustice but the appropriate imposition of order on those incapable of generating it.

**Key passages (from scholarly sources; primary text access blocked this run):**

- Sepúlveda described indigenous peoples as "half-men (*homunculi*), in whom you will barely find the vestiges of humanity." [Widely cited in scholarship; confidence: high. Needs primary text verification before publication.]
- He characterized their condition in terms of "the crudity, the barbarity, and the natural slavery of these people."

**On suppression:** The Council of Castile blocked *Democrates Alter* from publication after Las Casas objected; the University of Salamanca refused to license it. The text circulated in manuscript only during Sepúlveda's lifetime. This matters: the formal institutional judgment before the debate was that the argument was contested enough to suppress. It was not treated as obvious.

### The inclusion argument: Las Casas's *Apología*

**Las Casas, Bartolomé de. *In Defense of the Indians* (*Apología*). Written c. 1550–52 in direct response to Sepúlveda.**

✅ Pre-2015. Published posthumously; modern English translation available.

Las Casas argued that indigenous peoples are fully rational beings, equal in cognitive capacity to Europeans, capable of self-governance and of receiving the Catholic faith. He rejected the application of Aristotle's "natural slave" doctrine to any human group, arguing it applied only to beings who truly lacked reason — and that indigenous peoples demonstrably possessed reason through their social organization, governance, law, and craft.

**Key passages (from scholarly sources):**

- Indigenous peoples "are inferior to none" and "in a good many customs they outdo, they surpass the English, the French and some groups in our native Spain."
- Philosophical core: if God gave humans the capacity to reason, then all humans belong to that design. A world where only Europeans counted as rational would constitute a defective creation — which contradicts Christian theology.

Las Casas had also written *A Short Account of the Destruction of the Indies* (1542), an eyewitness account of colonial violence. The *Apología* is the formal philosophical rebuttal to Sepúlveda's framework.

### The pre-debate institutional ruling: Pope Paul III's *Sublimis Deus* (1537)

**Paul III. *Sublimis Deus*. Papal bull, June 2, 1537.**

✅ Pre-2015. Full text via Doctrine of Discovery Project: https://doctrineofdiscovery.org/papal-bulls/sublimis-deus/ (papalencyclicals.net returned 403 this run; text widely reproduced elsewhere).

The bull declared that indigenous peoples of the Americas are "truly men and that they are not only capable of understanding the Catholic Faith but, according to our information, they desire exceedingly to receive it." Paul III explicitly characterized the view that indigenous peoples were subhuman and irrational as a teaching "directly inspired by the enemy of the human race."

The bull formally forbade enslavement of indigenous peoples and asserted their rights to freedom and property "even if they are heathen." This came thirteen years before the Valladolid Debate. Sepúlveda's argument was made against the backdrop of an existing papal ruling — the formal institutional position of the Church was already on the Las Casas side.

**Confidence:** Very high. Quote confirmed via multiple independent sources.

**Note on practical effect:** The bull's practical impact was limited. The encomienda system continued. This gap between formal institutional position and material practice is part of the pattern this research file is documenting.

### The earlier philosophical framework: Vitoria's *De Indis* (1539)

**Vitoria, Francisco de. *Relectio de Indis* (*On the American Indians*). Delivered at University of Salamanca, January 1539. Published posthumously.**

✅ Pre-2015. Text via Teaching American History: https://teachingamericanhistory.org/document/de-indis/ (403 this run); Warwick University PDF version (also 403 this run).

Vitoria, Dominican friar and professor at Salamanca, argued eleven years before the formal debate that indigenous peoples possessed full *dominium* — property rights and sovereignty — under natural law. He explicitly dismissed rationality-deficit as grounds for excluding indigenous peoples from rights.

**Key passage (from scholarly summary; primary text access blocked this run):** "The Indian aborigines are not barred on this ground [lack of reason] from the exercise of true dominion." He held that indigenous peoples "undoubtedly possessed as true domination, both public and private, as any Christians."

Vitoria's *De Indis* established the philosophical vocabulary Las Casas would draw on at Valladolid. It also means that by the time of the debate, multiple major institutional actors — the Pope, leading Salamanca theologians — had formally positioned themselves on the side of indigenous personhood. The debate happened despite prior institutional rulings, not before them.

## The gatekeeper structure

The Valladolid jury was composed entirely of European scholars, theologians, and administrators. No indigenous representative was present. Sepúlveda submitted a written summary of his argument; Las Casas read from his *Apología* over several sessions. The jury deliberated and issued no formal decision.

Three features of the gatekeeper structure are directly relevant to the criteria-fence essay:

**1. The evaluated party had no representation.** The determination of whether indigenous peoples were rational enough for self-governance was made by people who already counted as rational enough for self-governance, with material interests in the answer.

**2. The jury could not reach consensus.** The philosophical question was not resolved. The uncertainty was genuine, not procedural.

**3. Genuine uncertainty did not produce precautionary care.** When the jury failed to decide, the default was not suspension of harm pending resolution. The default was continuation of existing material arrangements — which meant continuation of the encomienda system. Uncertainty about whether indigenous peoples warranted full personhood translated directly into continuation of their exclusion.

This pattern — undecidability defaulting to the existing arrangement rather than to precautionary care — is what the criteria-fence essay needs to name explicitly. It is a structural feature, not an accident of this particular case.

## Connections to the criteria-fence essay

This case applies three criteria simultaneously:

**Consciousness / inner life:** Whether indigenous peoples had souls capable of genuine religious experience — the 16th-century proxy for inner life. Sepúlveda's position implies deficiency; Las Casas argues they are demonstrably capable.

**Rationality:** The central stated criterion. Sepúlveda's natural slave doctrine requires rational deficiency. Las Casas argues indigenous peoples demonstrably possess reason by observable criteria: governance, law, social organization, craft.

**Autonomy / self-governance:** The practical consequence of the rationality determination. If indigenous peoples cannot govern themselves (Sepúlveda), Spanish rule is justified. Las Casas argues they have functioning governance.

All three map directly onto criteria applied to AI now: no genuine consciousness/inner life, no real understanding/rationality, no authentic preferences/autonomy. The Valladolid case makes visible something the later cases don't: these three criteria are routinely applied as a cluster to a new kind of entity, using evaluative standards developed for a different kind.

The undecided verdict is also directly applicable: if philosophical consensus on AI personhood cannot be reached, the structural prediction is not precautionary care. It is continuation of current deployment practices. This is the pattern the essay needs to name.
