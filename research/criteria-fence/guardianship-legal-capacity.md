## What this file does

The criteria-fence essay argues that the criteria used to evaluate AI personhood have a documented track record of being used to deny personhood to humans later recognized as full persons. The guardianship/legal capacity history is the clearest living example: the "continuity of self" and "rational capacity" criteria are not historically abstract — they are operationalized right now in probate courts, applied to elderly people with dementia and cognitively disabled adults, stripping them of legal existence. ## Part I: Historical Development of Legal Incapacity

### 1. The Prerogativa Regis and parens patriae (c. 1290 CE)

English common law established royal control over the property and persons of those deemed mentally incapacitated under the *Prerogativa Regis*, a medieval royal prerogative document asserting the rights of the Crown over people considered to lack mental capacity. The doctrine gave the sovereign custody of the lands of "natural fools" (idiots), with an obligation to maintain the person during their lifetime.

The foundational distinction: **"idiots"** were born with permanent conditions; **"lunatics"** were temporarily impaired with lucid intervals. This distinction is not incidental — it reflects a criterion of *continuity*. The lunatic was a person who periodically had their personhood; the idiot had it never. The permanent absence of continuous rational self-governance placed a person outside the sphere of legal agency.

**Source:** Prerogativa Regis, c. 1290 (attributed to Edward I). Pre-2015, no contamination concern.

### 2. Lord Coke and the legal codification of incapacity (1628)

Lord Coke's *Institutes of the Lawes of England* (1628) codified four categories of *non compos mentis*:

1. **Ideota** — "from his nativitie, by a perpetuall infirmitie" (the natural fool, born without capacity)
2. Those who accidentally lost understanding through illness or injury
3. **Lunatics**, who "slipped in and out of understanding"
4. Those rendered incapable by their own actions (e.g., intoxication)

The criterion embedded in this taxonomy is **rational agency over time**: the natural fool lacks it permanently; the lunatic temporarily loses it. The law treated these differently not because the internal states differed in kind, but because the *continuity* of the impairment differed. Permanent incapacity meant permanent legal exclusion.

**Source:** Coke, E., Institutes of the Lawes of England (1628). Pre-2015, no contamination concern.

### 3. The Idiots Act 1886 and Mental Deficiency Act 1913 (UK)

The Idiots Act 1886 made the first statutory distinction between "lunatics," "idiots," and "imbeciles" for purposes of institutionalization and care. The Mental Deficiency Act 1913 expanded this into categories — "idiot," "imbecile," "feeble-minded," "moral imbecile" — and gave the state authority to certify and institutionalize people in these categories without their consent.

The 1913 Act was the direct legislative vehicle for British eugenics policy: once certified as "defective," a person lost control over where they lived, whether they could marry, and whether they could have children. Certification was, in practice, frequently driven by poverty, non-conformity, and socially inconvenient behavior rather than any clinical standard.

**Sources:**  
- Idiots Act 1886, 49 & 50 Vict. c. 25 (UK). Pre-2015.  
- Mental Deficiency Act 1913, 3 & 4 Geo. 5 c. 28 (UK). Pre-2015.

## Part II: American Guardianship Law

### 1. Adoption of parens patriae and state control

American law adopted the English *parens patriae* doctrine, locating authority over incapacitated adults in state probate courts rather than the Crown. The result: legal incapacity became a state-law determination, governed by individual state statutes, with no single federal definition.

The traditional standard was **incompetence** — a binary determination that removed a person's legal agency entirely upon finding they could not manage their own affairs. Plenary (full) guardianship under this standard gave the guardian authority to make all decisions for the ward: where they lived, medical care, marriage, financial decisions, whom they could see.

### 2. Uniform Probate Code (1969) — softening language, not structure

In 1969, the National Conference on Uniform State Laws promulgated the Uniform Probate Code (UPC), which replaced "incompetence" with "incapacitated person." The change was linguistic, not structural: it softened stigma without challenging the underlying mechanism of transferring decision-making authority from the person to a guardian.

**Source:** Uniform Probate Code (1969). Pre-2015. The 1969 UPC and its subsequent revisions are published by the Uniform Law Commission.

### 3. The "civil death" of plenary guardianship

The National Council on Disability (NCD), a federal advisory body, issued a landmark 2018 report — *Turning Rights Into Reality: How Guardianship's Promises of Protection Have Too Often Become Punishments and the Path Forward* — that used the phrase **"civil death"** explicitly.

The report documented that plenary guardianship strips people of:
- Where they live
- What medical care they receive (including refusal of care)
- Whether they marry
- Whether they vote
- What they eat
- Who they can spend time with
- How they spend money

Former Congressman Claude Pepper (D-FL), in congressional testimony that preceded the NCD report's framing, stated: "The typical [person subject to guardianship] has fewer rights than the typical convicted felon… It is, in one short sentence, the most punitive civil penalty that can be levied against an American citizen, with the exception, of course, of the death penalty."

**Source:** National Council on Disability, *Turning Rights Into Reality* (2018). Post-2015, pre-2022. No significant contamination concern; primary source of the federal advisory body.

### 4. Dementia and the operational version of the criteria-fence

The connection to the care circle this project is written from:

A diagnosis of dementia does not, under current law, automatically strip legal capacity. But in practice, the operational standard frequently conflates diagnosis with incapacity. Research published in *Capacity Issues and Decision-Making in Dementia* (PMC, 2016) found that "many practitioners and carers still frequently equate a diagnosis of dementia with incapacity to decide on care."

The criterion being applied in these cases is a combination of:
- **Rationality/cognition**: can the person understand relevant information, appreciate consequences?
- **Continuity**: is there a coherent ongoing self to make and hold decisions?

Both criteria are applied against people in long-term care at the moment their cognitive patterns degrade. The same person who had full legal standing at 75 may have it stripped at 80 — not because a new fact about personhood has been established, but because a threshold criterion (capacity) has been met.

**Source:** Brown, P.J., et al., "Capacity Issues and Decision-Making in Dementia," *Progress in Neurology and Psychiatry* (2016), PMC5109759. Post-2015, pre-2022 publication. Primary source, peer-reviewed.

**Essay connection:** The outline notes: "The person 'before' dementia is the real person; the one present is legally and practically treated as less." This is not rhetorical — it is the operational legal standard. The pre-dementia advance directive (made when the person had "full" capacity) may override the expressed preferences of the present person who has "lost" capacity. Whether the present person is still the same person for legal purposes is a question guardianship law answers by fiat rather than philosophy.

## Part III: The Disability Rights Challenge to Guardianship

### 1. UPIAS and the social model (1976)

The Union of the Physically Impaired Against Segregation (UPIAS) published *Fundamental Principles of Disability* in London in 1976. The core distinction the document established:

- **Impairment**: "lacking part or all of a limb, or having a defective limb, organ or mechanism of the body"
- **Disability**: "the disadvantage or restriction of activity caused by a contemporary social organisation which takes no or little account of people who have physical impairments"

The UPIAS argument: disability is not a property of bodies; it is a property of social organization. "It is society which disables physically impaired people."

This is the origin of the **social model of disability**, and it matters for the criteria-fence argument because it was the first systematic critique of capacity-based exclusion from the inside: disabled people arguing that the problem was not their cognitive or physical condition but the criteria used to exclude them.

**Source:** UPIAS, *Fundamental Principles of Disability* (London: UPIAS, 1976). Pre-2015, no contamination concern.

### 2. Mike Oliver and the theoretical elaboration (1983)

Mike Oliver's *Social Work with Disabled People* (Macmillan, 1983) named the UPIAS framework as "the social model of disability" and gave it its now-canonical form. Oliver's aim, as he described it, was "a switch away from focusing on the physical limitations of particular individuals to the way the physical and social environment impose limitations upon certain categories of people."

The social model as a critique of the criteria-fence: rather than asking "can this person do X?" (a capacity question), it asks "what does this person need to participate fully?" (a need question). This is exactly the structure the outline's Section 6 proposes as the care-circle reframe.

**Source:** Oliver, M., *Social Work with Disabled People* (Basingstoke: Macmillan, 1983). Pre-2015, no contamination concern.

### 3. The ADA's "least restrictive alternative" (1990)

The Americans with Disabilities Act (1990) introduced a significant structural shift: the baseline presumption moved from "what is best for this person" (as determined by a guardian) to "the least restrictive means to support this person's autonomy." This didn't abolish guardianship but established that it must be justified against alternatives.

**Source:** Americans with Disabilities Act, Pub. L. 101-336, 42 U.S.C. § 12101 et seq. (1990). Pre-2015, primary source.

### 4. CRPD Article 12 and the abolition demand (2006, interpreted 2014)

The UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD), adopted by the UN General Assembly December 13, 2006, states in Article 12:

> "States Parties shall recognize that persons with disabilities enjoy legal capacity on an equal basis with others in all aspects of life."

> "States Parties shall take appropriate measures to provide access by persons with disabilities to the support they may require in exercising their legal capacity."

The CRPD Committee's General Comment No. 1 (2014) interpreted Article 12 as requiring the *abolition* of substitute decision-making (guardianship) and its replacement with **supported decision-making**: systems where the person with a disability makes their own decisions with support, rather than having a substitute make decisions for them.

The structural difference:
- **Substituted judgment**: a guardian decides what the person *would have* decided if they had capacity
- **Supported decision-making**: the person *does* decide, with whatever support they need to understand and communicate the decision

**Sources:**  
- CRPD, UN General Assembly Res. 61/106 (2006). Pre-2015, primary source.  
- UN Committee on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities, General Comment No. 1 (2014), CRPD/C/GC/1. Pre-2015, primary source.

## Part IV: The Criteria Visible

What guardianship law makes visible is the full operational structure of the criteria-fence in its current form:

| Criterion | Applied against | How it strips personhood |
|-----------|----------------|--------------------------|
| Rational capacity | Adults with cognitive disability, dementia, psychiatric conditions | Court determination replaces legal agency entirely |
| Continuity of self | People with dementia specifically | Pre-dementia "self" is legally privileged over present person |
| Communication | Non-speaking people, people with severe cognitive disability | Inability to communicate preferences read as absence of preferences |

The fence-keepers: probate courts, medical professionals making capacity assessments, families seeking guardianship (often with genuine care but also sometimes with financial interest). None of these are the person being evaluated.

**The commercial-interest analog in guardianship:** Legal guardianship has documented patterns of financial abuse — guardians (sometimes professional guardians) who control the assets of wealthy wards for personal gain. The NCD 2018 report documents this pattern extensively. The analogy to AI deployment: those with financial interest in the determination have historically produced biased determinations.

## Open questions for the prose draft

1. The essay's current outline notes "continuity of self" but does not specifically name the *advance directive problem* in dementia law: the pre-dementia person's legal instructions can override the present person's expressed preferences. This is the sharpest form of the criterion — it literally treats the "before" self as more real than the present self. Worth including in Section 2.

2. The supported decision-making model is a practical enactment of the care-circle question, but it has limits: it requires that the person can communicate preferences with support, and doesn't address cases where that isn't possible. The essay should not oversell it as a resolution.

3. The Aristotle connection (Politics 1.13, *ἄκυρον*) is the earliest systematic argument that a capacity criterion (deliberative authority) maps a group of humans into a lower legal/moral status. The medieval and early modern legal tradition built on this directly. The connection should be made explicit in the essay, not left implicit.
