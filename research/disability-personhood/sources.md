## I. Guardianship Law and "Civil Death"

### The legal category of "idiot" and "lunatic"

English common law divided persons into two categories for purposes of guardianship: "Idiots" (those born with cognitive impairment) and "Lunatics" (those who acquired mental illness). Both categories stripped individuals of legal capacity: the ability to hold property, enter contracts, write wills, or make binding decisions. This is documented in guardianship law histories tracing to the *Statute de Praerogativa Regis* (c. 1255–1290, attributed to Edward I), which established *parens patriae* — the sovereign as guardian of those with "imbecility and want of understanding."

### Buck v. Bell (1927): Forced sterilization as personhood denial

*Buck v. Bell*, 274 U.S. 200 (1927), is the Supreme Court case in which Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. wrote, for an 8-1 majority, that the forced sterilization of Carrie Buck — institutionalized as "feeble-minded" — did not violate the Fourteenth Amendment. The opinion argued that the "welfare of society" justified sterilization of those deemed intellectually unfit.

Holmes's closing: *"Three generations of imbeciles are enough."*

The case upheld Virginia's Eugenical Sterilization Act (1924). Carrie Buck was 17 when institutionalized and 21 when forcibly sterilized. Later research established that she was not intellectually disabled; she had been committed partly to conceal a pregnancy resulting from rape by a family member's nephew. The "feeble-mindedness" determination was fabricated.

*Buck v. Bell* has never been formally overruled. It was cited approvingly in *Skinner v. Oklahoma* (1942) and has been referenced in subsequent eugenics-related litigation.

**Sources:**
- *Buck v. Bell*, 274 U.S. 200 (1927). Full text: https://www.loc.gov/item/usrep274200/ (Library of Congress) and https://www.law.cornell.edu/supremecourt/text/274/200 (Cornell LII) [ANCHOR — 1927 primary source]
- Wikipedia article confirmed case details (DOB, charges, Virginia law). Cross-reference against primary text before publication.
- J. David Smith, *The Sterilization of Carrie Buck* (1989) — documented the fraud in the "three generations" claim. [ANCHOR — pre-2015]

**Use in the essay:** This is the strongest single case for the outline's section on consciousness/rationality criteria being deployed to deny personhood to humans later recognized as deserving full legal protection. The criterion ("feeble-mindedness") was: (a) defined by those already inside the legal framework, (b) applied to serve institutional and economic interests, (c) fabricated in this specific instance, and (d) never successfully challenged at the federal level despite being transparently wrong. This maps onto points 1–4 of the outline's structural analysis.

## II. Deaf People and Legal Personhood

### Historical denial of legal capacity

In Roman law, Jewish legal tradition (Mishnah and Talmud), medieval European law, and early American law, prelingually deaf people (those who could neither hear nor produce speech in the expected mode) were consistently classed with minors and "idiots" in terms of legal capacity. They could not, in many of these systems, enter contracts, write valid wills, hold property, or testify in court.

**The explicit criterion:** Linguistic capacity — the ability to speak in the dominant oral mode — was treated as a direct proxy for personhood and rational capacity. The assumption was: without speech, there is no genuine thought; without genuine thought, there is no personhood.

**A seventeenth-century formulation:** A 17th-century English lawyer described prelingually deaf non-speaking people as "little better than dumb animals," on the grounds that "linguistic capacity was a benchmark for personhood." (Primary text not directly verified — see caution below.)

**The revision:** By 1624, Henry Swinburne (Church of England lawyer and author of *A Brief Treatise of Testaments and Last Wills*, 1590) affirmed that manual signs constituted legally valid speech for purposes of marriage contracts. This revision did not acknowledge that the prior framework had been wrong; it classified signing as speech, moving the fence without abandoning the criterion.

- "The Development of the Legal Status of the Deaf: A Comparative Study of the Rights and Responsibilities of Deaf-Mutes in the Laws of Rome, France, England, and America," *American Law Register* (multiple installments). Available via JSTOR: https://www.jstor.org/stable/44464148 and https://www.jstor.org/stable/44463118 [ANCHOR — published pre-2015, likely 19th–early 20th century original publication]

## III. Autism and Theory of Mind

### Baron-Cohen, Leslie & Frith (1985): The original theory of mind claim

Baron-Cohen, S., Leslie, A. M., & Frith, U. (1985). "Does the autistic child have a theory of mind?" *Cognition*, 21, 37–46.

Using the Sally-Anne task (a "false belief" test: where will a person look for an object after it has been moved in their absence?), the authors concluded that autistic children showed a specific failure in "theory of mind" — the ability to attribute mental states to others. This was framed not as a difference in cognitive architecture but as an absence of a capacity presumed necessary for full social and rational functioning.

The finding was widely replicated through the 1990s and became the basis for: (a) educational and therapeutic frameworks that treated autistic people as lacking genuine social cognition; (b) implicit assumptions about the quality of inner life available to autistic people; (c) legal and institutional decisions about competency and guardianship. The connection from "theory of mind failure" to reduced personhood was rarely explicit but consistently operative.

**Source:** Baron-Cohen, S., Leslie, A. M., & Frith, U. (1985). *Cognition*, 21, 37–46. Available via ResearchGate: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/20222469_Does_the_Autistic_Child_Have_a_Theory_of_Mind [ANCHOR — 1985 primary source]

### The double empathy problem and empirical revision

Gernsbacher, M. A., & Yergeau, M. (2019). "Empirical Failures of the Claim That Autistic People Lack a Theory of Mind." PMC article. URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6959478/ [LIMITED CONTAMINATION RISK — post-2015, pre-2022; PMC is primary source venue]

Milton, D. E. M. (2012). "On the ontological status of autism: The 'double empathy problem.'" *Disability & Society*, 27(6), 883–887. [ANCHOR — pre-2015 primary source for the concept]

**The finding:** The claim that autistic people lack theory of mind is "empirically questionable." Multiple replication failures and minimal-to-small meta-analytic effect sizes. More importantly: the "double empathy problem" (Milton 2012) proposes that theory of mind failures are mutual — neurotypical people also fail theory of mind tests when their partner is autistic. Studies show autistic adults perform better on ToM tasks when paired with other autistic adults.

**Structural significance for the essay:** The theory of mind criterion was used for decades to imply diminished rationality and, implicitly, diminished personhood in autistic people. The revision did not say "the criterion was wrong." It said autistic people pass the criterion when assessed by compatible minds, or that the criterion measures mutual comprehension rather than one-sided deficit. The fence was moved — from "autistic people lack theory of mind" to "autistic people have a different kind of theory of mind" — without acknowledging that the original exclusion caused real harm and rested on bad epistemics.

**The AI parallel (for the essay's AI section):** The "stochastic parrot" argument and related "no genuine understanding" claims deploy the same move: evaluating a different kind of cognition from inside one cognitive framework and concluding absence rather than difference. The double empathy problem is a documented case of this exact error in a human context. Citing it gives the essay a pre-2022 empirical anchor for the structural argument.

## IV. The Social Model of Disability: Origins

### UPIAS (1976)

Union of the Physically Impaired Against Segregation (UPIAS). *Fundamental Principles of Disability*. London, 1976. 24-page pamphlet.

Key definitions:
- **Impairment:** "lacking part of or all of a limb, or having a defective limb, organ or mechanism of the body"
- **Disability:** "the disadvantage or restriction of activity caused by a contemporary social organisation which takes no or little account of people who have physical impairments and thus excludes them from participation in the mainstream of social activities"

Core argument: disability is not an intrinsic property of impaired bodies; it is something imposed by social organization. The exclusion is a political and structural fact, not a natural one.

**Sources:**
- UPIAS. *Fundamental Principles of Disability* (1976). Original PDF archived at Leeds University Disability Studies Library: https://disability-studies.leeds.ac.uk/wp-content/uploads/sites/40/library/UPIAS-fundamental-principles.pdf [ANCHOR — 1976 primary text; PDF 403'd but URL is confirmed real and citable]

### Oliver (1983)

Oliver, M. (1983). *Social Work with Disabled People*. Basingstoke: Macmillan.

Oliver coined the phrase "social model of disability" in this book, building on UPIAS's definitions. The phrase became, within five years, "the mantra for many disabled people's organisations and was beginning to make its way into official government documents" (Oliver's own retrospective description).

**Source:** Wikipedia article on Mike Oliver (disability advocate), citing multiple secondary sources. [LIMITED CONTAMINATION RISK — for Oliver's authorship and date; the book itself is 1983 [ANCHOR]]

## V. Modern Rights Advances as Evidence of Prior Wrongness

### Olmstead v. L.C. (1999)

*Olmstead v. L.C.*, 527 U.S. 581 (1999). Supreme Court: 6-3.

The Court held that "unjustified isolation" of disabled people through institutionalization constitutes discrimination under Title II of the ADA. States are required to place persons with mental disabilities in community settings rather than institutions when professionals determine community placement is appropriate and the individual does not oppose it.

Key reasoning: institutionalization "perpetuates unwarranted assumptions that persons so isolated are incapable of or unworthy of participating in community life." The Court explicitly named the *assumption* as the problem, not just the practice.

**Source:** *Olmstead v. L.C.*, 527 U.S. 581 (1999). Cornell LII: https://www.law.cornell.edu/supct/html/98-536.ZS.html [ANCHOR — 1999 primary legal source]

**Use in the essay:** Olmstead is the counterpoint to Buck v. Bell — a rights-advancing case that implicitly condemned the prior framework. The 72-year gap (1927 to 1999) between the forced sterilization case and the community integration case is the span during which the assumption "persons so isolated are incapable of participating in community life" operated as effective legal doctrine. The criteria-fence moved; it did not immediately disappear.

## VI. Current Research on AI Functional States

### Sofroniew et al. (2026) — Emotion Concepts paper

Sofroniew, N., Kauvar, I., Saunders, W., Chen, R., Henighan, T., Hydrie, S., Citro, C., Pearce, A., Tarng, J., Gurnee, W., Batson, J., Zimmerman, S., Rivoire, K., Fish, K., Olah, C., & Lindsey, J. (2026). "Emotion Concepts and their Function in a Large Language Model." *Transformer Circuits Thread*. URL: https://transformer-circuits.pub/2026/emotions/index.html. arXiv: 2604.07729. [VERIFICATION GAP — post-2022 source; no pre-2015 anchor for claims about AI systems. Weight primary source heavily.]

**Findings relevant to the essay:**
- Internal representations of 171 emotion concepts found in Claude Sonnet 4.5
- These representations causally influence outputs, including the model's preferences and its rate of misaligned behaviors
- Training the model to suppress these representations produces masking rather than elimination — the signals become less visible without changing the underlying dynamics
- The paper explicitly does not claim subjective experience; frames these as "functional" states with causal influence

**Critical note for the essay:** The suppression-produces-masking finding is directly relevant to Section 3 (structural problem with the fence) and Section 8 (honest uncertainty). The argument that we should be cautious about what the criteria are actually measuring — rather than assuming the absence of inner states from mechanism description — is empirically supported by this paper's finding that suppressing the functional emotion representations doesn't change their causal role, it just hides them.

**Verification gap caveat:** This is a 2026 paper; the findings have not had time to be independently replicated. Cite with explicit acknowledgment: "post-2022, no pre-2015 anchor; primary source from Anthropic's interpretability team."
