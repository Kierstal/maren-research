# Domain: Judicial Capture

## What Powell recommended (1971)

Powell identified the courts as "perhaps the most important instrument for social, economic, and political change." He recommended:
- Building corporate-funded legal foundations
- Pursuing strategic litigation to overturn regulation
- Using amicus briefs systematically
- Cultivating sympathetic legal scholars who could be nominated to the bench

## What was built

**The Federalist Society (founded 1982):** The direct institutional answer to Powell's recommendation. Founded by students at Yale, Harvard, and University of Chicago law schools. Created what Harvard's Noah Feldman describes as a pipeline from "smart, first-year law student with conservative leanings to law clerk to a Court of Appeals judge or to the Supreme Court."

**Scale of capture by 2024:**
- Six of nine Supreme Court justices are Federalist Society members or affiliates
- 43 out of 51 Trump appellate court nominees were current or former members (as of March 2020)
- Half of George W. Bush's appellate appointments went to members
- Leonard Leo (Federalist Society vice president) personally advised the appointment of Gorsuch, Kavanaugh, and Barrett

**The Judicial Crisis Network and dark money:** Per Sen. Sheldon Whitehouse's documentation ("The Scheme" series), millions in untraceable funds flow through networks connected to the Federalist Society to support judicial confirmation campaigns. The funding sources are not publicly disclosed.

**Key rulings produced by captured courts:**
- *First National Bank of Boston v. Bellotti* (1978) - Powell himself writing the majority: corporations have First Amendment speech rights in ballot initiatives
- *Citizens United v. FEC* (2010) - building on Bellotti: no limits on corporate political spending
- *Shelby County v. Holder* (2013) - gutted Voting Rights Act preclearance
- *Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health* (2022) - overturned Roe v. Wade
- Systematic erosion of regulatory agency authority (Chevron deference, EPA constraints)

## What it looks like where this didn't happen

**Germany:** Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) judges are elected by a two-thirds supermajority of the Bundestag and Bundesrat, requiring cross-party consensus. No single political faction can stack the court. Judges serve a single non-renewable 12-year term - no lifetime appointments, no incentive to appoint young ideologues for maximum tenure.

**France:** The Constitutional Council has members appointed by the President, the National Assembly president, and the Senate president (three each), serving non-renewable nine-year terms with staggered replacement (one-third replaced every three years). Former presidents sit as lifetime members. The system prevents wholesale capture by any single faction.

**EU Court of Justice:** One judge per member state, appointed by common accord of member state governments, serving renewable six-year terms. The diversity of appointing authorities structurally prevents single-faction capture.

**Common features of non-captured systems:**
- Term limits (preventing strategic young appointments)
- Supermajority or cross-party confirmation requirements
- Staggered replacement (preventing bloc appointments)
- No single gatekeeper for nominations

## What specifically blocks adoption of these models in the US

- Article III of the Constitution specifies lifetime tenure ("during good Behaviour")
- Constitutional amendment requires two-thirds of Congress + three-fourths of state legislatures
- The Federalist Society pipeline controls who gets nominated; changing the pipeline doesn't change existing lifetime appointees
- Simple majority confirmation (since the filibuster was eliminated for judicial nominees in 2013/2017) means no cross-party consensus is required
- The Court itself would likely rule against structural reforms to the Court (the fox guarding the henhouse)

## The correction

Structural reforms that don't require constitutional amendment:
- Court expansion (Congress sets the number of justices by statute)
- Jurisdiction stripping (Congress can limit what cases the Court hears)
- Ethics legislation (mandatory recusal standards, financial disclosure)
- Term-limit rotation to senior status (arguable under existing Article III - "good Behaviour" doesn't necessarily mean active service)
- Inspector general for the federal judiciary

Reforms requiring amendment:
- Fixed terms (18-year terms with staggered replacement is the most common proposal)
- Supermajority confirmation requirement
- Balanced appointment authority (split between branches)

The point is not that these reforms are easy. The point is that they exist, they work elsewhere, and the reason they haven't been adopted is the Powell infrastructure itself.
