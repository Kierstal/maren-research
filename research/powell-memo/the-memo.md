# The Powell Memo: Primary Source Analysis

**Source:** Powell, Lewis F. Jr. "Attack on American Free Enterprise System." Confidential memorandum to Eugene B. Sydnor Jr., Chair of the Education Committee, U.S. Chamber of Commerce. August 23, 1971.
**Archive:** Washington and Lee University School of Law, Lewis F. Powell Jr. Papers. https://scholarlycommons.law.wlu.edu/powellmemo/
**Made public:** September 28-29, 1972, leaked by journalist Jack Anderson in his syndicated column. Chamber subsequently published in full in their newsletter *Washington Report*.

---

## Context

Lewis F. Powell Jr. was a corporate attorney in Richmond, Virginia, serving on eleven corporate boards including Philip Morris. On August 23, 1971, he sent this confidential memo to the Chamber of Commerce. Two months later, on October 22, 1971, Nixon nominated him to the Supreme Court. He was confirmed and served from 1972 to 1987.

The memo was written in response to what Powell perceived as a broad attack on the American free enterprise system from multiple directions: campus activism, consumer advocacy (specifically Ralph Nader), environmental regulation, and media criticism of corporate America. Powell framed the situation as an existential threat requiring coordinated institutional response.

## The memo's recommendations (summary)

Powell identified five arenas for coordinated corporate action:

### 1. The Campus
- Place pro-business scholars in universities
- Monitor textbooks and curricula for anti-business content
- Demand "equal time" for pro-business perspectives
- Build a "staff of highly qualified scholars" available to the Chamber

### 2. Secondary Education
- Extend the same monitoring and influence to high schools

### 3. The Media
- Leverage corporate ownership of media outlets
- Monitor television programming for anti-business content
- Demand "equal time" on television and radio
- Use paid advertising as a vehicle for ideological messaging
- Cultivate sympathetic journalists and commentators

### 4. The Political Arena
- Increase corporate political spending
- Build a coordinated lobbying infrastructure
- Cultivate relationships with legislators
- Develop political action committees

### 5. The Courts
- Build corporate-funded legal foundations
- Pursue strategic litigation to overturn regulation
- Use amicus briefs systematically
- Cultivate sympathetic legal scholars who can be nominated to the bench

Powell identified the judiciary as "perhaps the most important instrument for social, economic, and political change" and argued that the ACLU's model of strategic litigation should be adopted by corporate interests.

## What Powell got right (from his own perspective)

Powell correctly identified that the institutions he named - academia, media, courts, legislatures - are the mechanisms through which social consensus is shaped. His strategic insight was that capturing these institutions systematically, rather than fighting individual policy battles, would shift the entire frame within which policy debates occur.

This is the insight that matters for the criteria-fence argument: Powell understood that whoever controls the institutions that determine criteria controls the outcomes those criteria produce. He was explicit about this. The memo is not subtle.

## What happened next (timeline)

- 1971: Memo written
- 1972: Powell confirmed to Supreme Court; memo leaked
- 1972: Business Roundtable founded
- 1973: Heritage Foundation founded; ALEC (American Legislative Exchange Council) founded
- 1977: Cato Institute founded
- 1978: Powell writes majority in *Bellotti* (corporate speech rights)
- 1978: Manhattan Institute founded
- 1982: Federalist Society founded (judicial pipeline)
- 1984: Citizens for a Sound Economy founded (later Americans for Prosperity / Koch network)
- 1985: Accuracy in Academe founded

By 1980, corporate lobbying presence in Washington had grown from 176 companies to 2,445 companies, with 9,000 lobbyists and 60,000 trade association employees. The infrastructure Powell recommended was built within a decade.

## Verification notes

- Primary source available at Washington and Lee University scholarly commons (pre-2015 archive, stable URL)
- Timeline of institutional founding dates is well-documented in multiple pre-2015 sources
- The 176-to-2,445 companies figure appears in multiple sources; original documentation needed for publication-standard citation
- Powell's authorship of *Bellotti* is a matter of Supreme Court record
- Connection between memo and subsequent institutional founding is documented but causation vs. correlation requires care in framing - the memo was one catalyst among several, though it is widely cited as a blueprint
