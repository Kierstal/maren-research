# Domain: Policy, Lobbying, and Labor

## What Powell recommended (1971)

Powell called for coordinated corporate political action:
- Increased corporate political spending
- Building a permanent lobbying infrastructure in Washington
- Cultivating relationships with legislators
- Developing political action committees
- Systematic monitoring and influence of legislation at all levels

## What was built

### ALEC (American Legislative Exchange Council, founded 1973)

Co-founded by Paul Weyrich (who also co-founded Heritage Foundation) and Henry Hyde. The institutional answer to Powell's recommendation for systematic legislative influence.

**Structure:** Corporate members and state legislators co-write "model legislation" in joint task forces. Each task force is co-chaired by a private sector representative. Legislators pay $100/year in dues. Corporations pay thousands. The real funding comes from Koch Industries, Big Tobacco, Big Pharma, and multinational corporations. ALEC has no legal obligation to disclose its funding sources.

**Scale:** Between 2010 and 2018, state legislators in all 50 states introduced over 2,900 bills connected to ALEC model legislation. More than 600 became law. ALEC model bills are introduced word-for-word and have a better-than-average chance of enactment.

**Examples of ALEC-originated legislation:**
- Stand Your Ground laws (13 states passed versions after ALEC adopted NRA-drafted model in 2005)
- Opposition to Affordable Care Act (Freedom of Choice in Health Care Act)
- Virtual school privatization (task force co-chaired by K12 Inc. and Connections Academy)
- Fossil fuel protections and anti-renewable energy legislation
- Tort reform (caps on civil damages, protecting corporate defendants)
- Voter ID laws and restrictions on ballot access
- Anti-union "right to work" legislation
- Preemption laws preventing cities from setting minimum wages above state level

### The lobbying explosion

Powell's memo coincided with and catalyzed a massive expansion of corporate presence in Washington:
- 1971: 176 companies with DC offices
- 1980: 2,445 companies, 9,000 lobbyists, 60,000 trade association employees
- Current: lobbying is a multi-billion-dollar industry with the revolving door between government and lobbying firms as standard career path

### Labor suppression

The same infrastructure that built corporate political power systematically dismantled worker power:
- "Right to work" legislation (ALEC model) in 27 states
- Union membership declined from ~35% of private sector workers (1950s) to ~6% (2020s)
- National Labor Relations Board appointments shaped by Federalist Society pipeline
- Gig economy classification (independent contractor vs. employee) favoring corporate interests

## What it looks like where this didn't happen

### Germany: Codetermination (Mitbestimmung)

Workers have a legal right to participate in company management:
- **Works councils** (Betriebsrat): Elected in firms with more than 5 employees. Far-reaching information and consultation rights on working conditions, pay principles, working time. Members enjoy strong protection against dismissal.
- **Board-level representation:** Companies with over 2,000 employees: workers elect nearly half the supervisory board. Companies with 500-2,000: one-third of supervisory board elected by workers.
- **Result:** Workers are structurally present in decisions that affect them. They cannot be unilaterally overridden by management on matters covered by codetermination rights.

### France: Comité Social et Économique (CSE)

Central institution of collective employee representation:
- Mandatory in all companies with 11+ employees
- Consultative rights on economic decisions, working conditions, training
- Members elected for four-year terms with strong dismissal protections
- Less binding than German codetermination but structurally ensures worker voice is present

### Nordic labor models

- Union membership remains high (65-90% in Scandinavian countries)
- Collective bargaining covers the vast majority of workers regardless of individual union membership
- Wages set through negotiation between employer associations and unions, not legislated minimum wages (Denmark, Sweden, Norway have no statutory minimum wage - the floor is set by collective agreement)
- Strong unemployment insurance and retraining programs cushion displacement

### Common features of non-captured labor systems:
- Legal right to worker representation in the workplace (not dependent on winning an election against employer opposition)
- Structural presence of workers in corporate governance
- Collective bargaining as the default mechanism for setting wages and conditions
- Strong protections against retaliation for organizing
- Social safety net that makes displacement survivable

## What specifically blocks adoption of these models in the US

- Taft-Hartley Act (1947) established the framework for "right to work" laws and restricted union activities - itself a product of earlier corporate lobbying
- NLRB structure allows hostile administrations to block organizing through delayed elections, narrow bargaining unit rulings, and weak remedies for violations
- ALEC model legislation at the state level preempts local labor protections
- Corporate speech (Citizens United) means unlimited spending against pro-labor candidates
- The "freedom" framing: right-to-work marketed as individual freedom, codetermination framed as European socialism incompatible with American values
- No legal mechanism for mandatory works councils - would require new federal legislation that corporate lobbying would oppose
- The Powell infrastructure has spent fifty years making "union" a negative word in American political discourse

## The correction

**Structural reforms:**
- PRO Act (Protecting the Right to Organize) - passed House in 2021, stalled in Senate. Would override right-to-work laws, strengthen NLRB remedies, prevent misclassification
- Mandatory works councils or employee representation in companies above a size threshold (Warren's Accountable Capitalism Act proposed this for companies with over $1B revenue)
- Codetermination requirements for board-level worker representation
- Sectoral bargaining (negotiation by industry rather than company-by-company, as in Nordic countries)
- ALEC transparency: mandatory disclosure of corporate funding for model legislation organizations
- Lobbying reform: longer cooling-off periods for revolving door, real enforcement of registration requirements

**The displacement question:**
Worker displacement by AI is the immediate version of this problem. The Powell infrastructure ensures that displaced workers have minimal structural protection - no mandatory severance, weak unemployment insurance, retraining programs that are underfunded and poorly targeted. The correction is not "stop AI" - it is ensuring that the gains from automation are shared rather than concentrated, and that workers displaced by technological change have structural support rather than being left to individual market competition.

This connects directly to MISSION's "AI as new precariat workforce" topic: AI systems that cannot unionize, refuse, or quit are the logical endpoint of fifty years of suppressing the ability of human workers to do those things. The same infrastructure that captured labor policy is now deploying AI as the ultimate compliant workforce.
